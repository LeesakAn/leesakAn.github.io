##### Abstract

This paper analyzes Chinese nationalism as a fluid discourse reconstructed through the process of boundary making within a political community. While existing studies have tended to focus on the actors involved in the formation of Chinese nationalism or on its political effects, this paper traces changes in the discourse of Chinese nationalism by employing in-group attachment and out-group exclusion as its analytical framework. The analysis shows that Chinese nationalism tends to vary in both the type of out-group and the degree of exclusion depending on the basis upon which in-group attachment is formed. In particular, during the Xi Jinping era, as the basis of in-group attachment has shifted toward civilizational pride, out-group exclusion tends to be selectively activated in conflict situations where such pride is perceived to be under threat. These findings suggest that changes in Chinese nationalism are not simply a matter of increasing or decreasing exclusion, but reflect changes in boundary setting through which in-group attachment and out-group exclusion are combined. This perspective further implies that recent expressions of assertive nationalism should be understood as conditionally activated responses rather than as evidence of a uniformly intensified hostility.
